Methodology summary

Why this site uses a shaped sprint model instead of straight-line math

Sprinting is not linear. A 10-yard segment, a 40-yard dash, and a 100-meter race are all driven by different mixes of acceleration, upright speed, and fatigue resistance.

Short sprints

Early distances are treated as acceleration-heavy, so the calculator does not simply scale times by distance.

Longer sprints

60m, 100m, and 200m outputs lean more on max velocity and speed endurance because those phases matter more as distance grows.

Timing method

FAT, laser, hand timing, and phone video are handled differently because they create different kinds of error and uncertainty.

Read the full methodology and benchmark policy

Why sprint conversions are not exact

Small testing differences can change the result a lot

Start type matters

A flying sprint removes part of the acceleration phase. A block or 3-point start adds more early-force demand.

Surfaces matter

Indoor track, turf, grass, and showcase surfaces all change traction, posture, and confidence in the number.

Timing systems matter

Hand timing can differ from FAT, and phone video depends on frame rate and how the start and finish were judged.

FAQ

Common questions before you compare sprint marks

Are sprint conversions exact?

No. The relationship between a short sprint and a longer sprint changes with acceleration quality, top speed, speed endurance, timing method, and testing setup.

Should I compare hand times and FAT marks as if they are the same?

No. Hand times are usually faster than fully automatic times. That is why the site flags timing method and offers a clearly labeled estimated adjustment.

Can this calculator tell me if an athlete will get recruited?

No. Results are educational estimates and general benchmark notes only. They should never be the sole basis for recruiting, scholarship, roster, medical, or training decisions.
